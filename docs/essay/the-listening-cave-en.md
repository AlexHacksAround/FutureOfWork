# The Listening Cave — The Office of 2035

## Lascaux, seventeen thousand years ago

Seventeen thousand years ago, in the limestone hills above the Vézère river, people went to work in the dark.

They carried lamps of hollowed sandstone filled with tallow, wicks of juniper. They climbed down into the cold of the earth and built wooden scaffolds — the sockets they cut for the beams are still there in the rock. They ground their pigments by hand: red and yellow ochre, manganese black. Then, in chambers no daylight had ever touched, they painted. Aurochs five meters long. Horses in mid-stride. Stags swimming a river with their heads held high. Nearly two thousand figures at Lascaux alone, laid down over centuries.[^1]

Call the place what it was: a workplace. Nobody lived in those chambers — the hearths and bones of daily life are near the entrances, not in the deep galleries. The deep galleries were somewhere you went, on purpose, with tools and training, to do work that mattered to everyone. The wall held the group's knowledge: which animals, which seasons, which movements of the herds. The wall taught — generations of hands learned the craft by working beside better hands. The wall hosted ritual, the gathering that turned individuals into a people. And the wall was collaborative in the deepest sense: painters worked over one another's figures for centuries, layer upon layer, no signatures, no owner. The oldest shared workspace we know of is a cave wall, and it worked so well we still recognize ourselves in it.

## From stone to screen

Since then, we have designed our tools to store data and work with information the same way: stones to write on or carve into, papyrus, punch cards, paper, screens. Touchscreens come very close to the cave wall again — only now the wall becomes interactive.

Each step in that line changed who could work with information and how fast it moved. Punch cards counted the United States census of 1890 in a fraction of the expected time and became the memory of the first data machines.[^2] Paper made knowledge cheap enough to file everywhere; screens made it fast enough to change every second. But one thing never changed in seventeen thousand years: the surface stayed dumb. Stone, papyrus, card, paper, glass — the surface holds the marks, and a human being does all the understanding. Every generation of tools moved the marks faster. None of them ever understood a word.

## A universal interface

That is what is different now. Thanks to the rapid advancement in natural language processing and the rise of large language models, information systems can not only understand and produce natural language — they can become a universal and dynamic interface to programs, functions, data and other information systems.

Follow that one step further and the idea of "software" itself comes apart. Today an application is a fixed set of screens, designed years before your moment of need, identical for everyone. A system that understands both language and task does not need pre-built screens. It can generate the interface at the moment the task appears — a form for exactly the decision at hand, a chart of exactly the comparison under argument, controls for exactly the thing being adjusted — and discard it when the task is done. The interface stops being a product somebody shipped and becomes an answer the system gives. Keep that idea in hand; both technologies at the heart of this essay depend on it.

Right now, we mostly apply this small-scale, on personal computing devices: a chat window on a phone, a code editor that writes alongside you. What would a work environment and culture look like in which we take what is possible, evolve the technologies further and create a completely new way of "working"?

## Linear evolution?

Close your eyes and picture "the office of the future." Be honest about what appears.

A glass tower, probably. A lobby the temperature of a server room. A hologram receptionist flickering politely at the door, and somewhere behind it a humanoid assistant gliding across polished stone with a tray. Walls of dashboards, everything rendered in that particular blue that no one ever chose but everyone accepts. Perhaps an avatar of a colleague, beamed into a meeting room, smiling at nothing.

This picture is not a forecast. It is a rerun. It is the office of the future as imagined around 1968, re-rendered every decade since with better graphics and the same assumptions: that progress means more screens, more glass, more glow, and machines that imitate people. It extrapolates the recent past and calls the result tomorrow.

The actual history of technology tells a different story, and it was written down clearly in 1991, when Mark Weiser of Xerox PARC opened the founding essay of ubiquitous computing with a sentence worth pinning above every workplace-strategy meeting: "The most profound technologies are those that disappear. They weave themselves into the fabric of everyday life until they are indistinguishable from it."[^3] Weiser and his colleagues called the goal calm technology — systems that live in the periphery of attention and step forward only when needed, the way a good room does, rather than shouting for focus the way every screen must.

Look around and you will see that disappearance, not accumulation, is what mature technology does. Your home contains dozens of electric motors and you could not point to most of them. Writing covers every surface of your life and you do not call it "text technology." The technologies that changed us most are the ones we stopped noticing. The glowing rectangle is not the end state of computing; it is computing's adolescence: the stage where the machine still demands that you look at it.

And the machines that imitate people are a dead end of a different kind. A face that is almost a face triggers alarm, not trust. We have known about the uncanny valley for fifty years, and no rendering budget has ever crossed it. Worse, an artificial colleague makes a social promise it cannot keep. It asks for the attention we owe to persons and returns the judgment of a system. A hologram at the front desk is a receptionist minus everything that made them good at their job. The uncanny valley is not an engineering gap waiting to be closed. It is a signpost that says: wrong direction.

So reject the rerun, all of it — the glass, the glow, the dashboards turned into wallpaper, the synthetic staff. The question for 2035 is not what technology to put into the office. It is what the technology finally allows to disappear. What remains when it does is the subject of the rest of this essay: two technologies — a wall you can write on and a table you can feel — and the rooms they make possible. Each of those rooms exists today as a place you can walk through, in 360 degrees, at the link in its chapter.

## The impact of automation

User interfaces are one vector along which technology will change the way we interact with information systems. The other vector is what we actually do with those systems. The automation of today allows us to eliminate repetitive tasks: manual labour performed with the help of a keyboard and a mouse. This wave of automation touches jobs which compare to work on a manufacturing line, where things were once assembled by hand until machines removed those tasks. Automation with AI tools will remove the tasks in which your specific human capabilities do not add value. The future of work is work in which humans add a value a machine cannot. That is nothing new either — it is simply another industrial revolution.

What that remaining value is can be said plainly: judgment under uncertainty, responsibility for a decision, taste, trust between people, and the conversation that changes someone's mind. Everything in the office of 2035 should exist to make those things easier, because they are the product now.

But what does this mean for office buildings? What will our workplaces look like if we do not need to enter data or use screens anymore?

## Let's write on the walls again

*Walk this room: https://futureofwork.byatw.com/#painted-wall*

The first technology is the interactive wall. The wall can display information and is touchable; the room listens as well and can answer. Imagine rooms in which you can lounge, stand or sit, take in information on all the walls, and work on that data together as a group.

Picture one such room. Three of its walls work. They do not glow — the surface is matte and reads like paper in daylight, and when nobody is working, the walls are simply walls: wood, plaster, a window onto the street. Then a team walks in with a problem — say, the new pump is late. The walls take the work. The schedule spreads across one. The pump stands on another as a full-scale engineering drawing, full scale because the wall is the one display large enough for it. The open questions collect on the third, in handwriting, because a finger is a pen here.

Everything shown is touchable. Pull the schedule sideways with a flat hand and it scrolls through the quarter. Circle a bearing on the drawing and its tolerances step out of the wall next to your hand. And the room listens. Ask, "show the version before we moved the inlet," and the earlier drawing hangs itself beside the current one. Ask what changed, and the differences trace themselves in a second colour. Nobody typed. Nobody searched for the file.

Notice what is missing: an application. Nobody booked a CAD seat or shared a spreadsheet window; there is no software "on" this wall in the old sense at all. The model behind the room understands the task and generates the interface the task needs, at the moment it is needed — a timeline while the talk is about dates, a cost breakdown when it turns to money, drawn where the drawing was. This is the universal interface from the opening chapters, grown to the size of architecture. Software stops being a product you open and becomes a capability the room renders.

The deepest change is social. Today almost every meeting has an operator: the person with the laptop, driving, while everyone else watches a projection and dictates edits. At the wall there is no driver's seat. Whoever points, works. Whoever asks, gets an answer. Six people can stand at six parts of the same problem at once, and their work accumulates in layers the wall remembers — who changed what, when, and what it looked like before — the way the painters at Lascaux worked over one another's animals for centuries without erasing them.

That is what the first technology buys: a room where the group's full attention lands on the problem and on each other, and none of it is spent operating a computer.

## Let's feel data

*Walk this room: https://futureofwork.byatw.com/#loom*

The second technology is a surface like a table which not only displays data — Microsoft built a table that could do that as early as 2008[^4] — but which allows you to *feel* the data. A small deviation in the result of a measurement becomes a little bump under your fingertips, which you feel before you see it. You want to dive into it, so you press on the bump and zoom it open on the table, down to the raw numbers.

Why a table? Because a table is where people stand together. It is waist-high; you walk up to it, lean over it, colleagues stand across from you, and the data lies between you the way engineering drawings lay between engineers for two centuries. A table you stand around has no head.

And why touch? Because sight is the channel we have saturated. Every chart in the building competes for the same pair of eyes, and the eye reads a surface serially — it looks here, then there. The hand works in parallel. Fingertips resolve height differences of a fraction of a millimetre without any attention at all; you notice a crumb on a tabletop while talking to someone. Put that spare bandwidth to work: sweep your palm across this quarter's production runs, and the one batch that drifted meets your hand as a ridge. You did not search for it. It found you.

None of this is fantasy. Hiroshi Ishii's group at MIT has pursued exactly this goal for over a decade under the name radical atoms: material whose shape computation can change.[^5] Their inFORM table renders data in physical relief with a bed of motorised pins.[^6] Its successor Materiable gives such a surface simulated material behaviour, so that it yields, springs back or resists when you press it.[^7] And in Bristol, researchers showed in 2013 that focused ultrasound can place pressure points on a bare hand in mid-air, above an ordinary surface.[^8] The pieces of the haptic table exist in laboratories today. What 2035 requires is not an invention. It is integration, and nine years of engineering.

There is also an old debt repaid at this table. Computing began at one. The loom Jacquard built in 1804 read punched cards to weave patterns into silk;[^9] those cards became the memory of the first data machines, and Ada Lovelace explained the first computer by pointing back at the loom: the Analytical Engine "weaves algebraical patterns just as the Jacquard-loom weaves flowers and leaves."[^10] Working with data began as a craft — patterns, hands, a machine you stood at. Then it disappeared behind glass for eighty years. The table brings it back out.

The interface follows the same rule as the wall: it is generated, not installed. Lay a dataset on the table and the table renders the workbench that dataset needs — sliders sized for fingers, filters for the columns that matter, a comparison view the moment you set two series side by side. Nobody ever wrote "haptic quarterly-report software." The capability is composed at the moment of need and gone when you are done.

What changes for people is who gets to find things. Today an anomaly is found by the one person paid to stare at that chart, and then it travels the company as a screenshot. On the table, the deviation is public and physical: three people feel the same bump, disagree about it, press it open together and read the raw numbers where they stand. The data stops being a report someone shows you. It becomes a thing on the table — which is where hard questions have always been put.

## Interaction

*Walk this room: https://futureofwork.byatw.com/#hearth*

Groups meet in settings which allow everybody to see each other. Data is displayed on the walls. The interaction happens naturally.

Those three sentences sound modest until you compare them with what we build today. The standard meeting room is arranged around a screen at one end. Every chair faces it, and for an hour, colleagues see mostly the sides of each other's heads while one person talks at a projection with their back half-turned. The room is built for the display, and the people are arranged around its needs. The other standard answer, the open-plan floor, promised interaction by exposure and delivered the opposite: when firms removed the walls, face-to-face interaction fell by roughly seventy percent, and electronic messaging rose to replace it.[^11] Being visible is not the same as being in conversation.

The room of 2035 gets the geometry right because the technology finally permits it. When every wall can carry the data, no wall is the front. People sit and stand in an arc or a circle and see faces. Nobody presents: the material stands on the walls around the group, and anyone can walk up to it, point at it, change it. Questions go to the room and the room answers — a number checked, a document found, a chart drawn — without anyone breaking away to a keyboard. The record keeps itself: what was decided, and what the walls showed at the moment it was decided.

A meeting is, at bottom, a device for reading people. A number can be sent in a message; judgment, doubt and commitment have to be seen. When the data moves onto the walls and the operating of computers disappears, what is left in the middle of the room is exactly what the room is for — people, facing each other, deciding. That is interaction happening naturally, and it is the entire point of paying for a room.

## Focus

*Walk this room: https://futureofwork.byatw.com/#den*

When people want to focus alone or in a small group, well-lit rooms with interactive walls allow them to retreat for some time. The documentation is created by the system.

Two details in that sentence carry the weight. The first is *well-lit*. The focus room of 2035 is not a dark cocoon; it may be the brightest, calmest room on the floor — daylight, wood, a plant, a door that closes. What makes it a focus room is not gloom but subtraction. The wall carries one problem and nothing else: no inbox, no status feeds, none of the ambient noise that follows a laptop into every room. You retreat here not to hide from work but to meet a single hard piece of it, with the one condition knowledge work almost never gets — a long, uninterrupted run at it.

The second is *the documentation*. The heaviest tax on focused work has always been its paperwork: you think for two hours, then spend one writing down what you thought so the team can use it. Here the room does the clerical part. It listens, and it keeps the working record — the decisions land in a log, the sketch on the wall becomes a figure, and when you open the door, the summary and the open questions are already drafted and filed where the team will look tomorrow. You review the record instead of writing it. The same holds for two or three people working a problem together in a small room: the session ends and leaves behind a document nobody had to write.

The scarcest resource in any company is uninterrupted human thought. This room is where the building admits that.

## One lease away

2035 sounds distant. It is one office lease away. A standard ten-year lease signed this year runs into a world where the wall works and the table pushes back — and it will be the same building, because the offices in use nine years from now are the offices standing today. Everything in this essay is a retrofit question, not a construction project.

Three questions worth an hour of leadership time:

1. How much of the space you pay for is shaped around screens and data entry — work that will not exist in that form in 2035?
2. If software becomes something rooms render rather than something employees operate, what will people come to your office to do that they could not do anywhere else?
3. Which of your rooms would people still walk into if nobody required it?

The office described here already exists in pixels. You can walk through it, in 360 degrees, starting at the street door: https://futureofwork.byatw.com/#approach — the wall, the table and the rooms above are all there. It makes a better conversation starter than a slide deck. Take a colleague.
